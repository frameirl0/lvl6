Beginner Mistakes
in the Gym

6 min read March 2026 LVL VI Coaching Team

Starting the gym is one of the best decisions you can make. But most beginners make the same avoidable mistakes, and those mistakes cost them months of real progress. This guide is the shortcut we wish more people had on day one.

01 Following Random Programs from Social Media

This is probably the single most common mistake. You find a workout video on YouTube or a plan from a fitness influencer, try it for two weeks, then switch to something else when you stop seeing results. Repeat, indefinitely, with nothing to show for it.

The problem isn't the individual programs. The problem is the switching. No program can produce results in two weeks. Transformation requires months of consistent work on a single, structured plan. Progress isn't always visible, but it's always happening if you stay on the program long enough for it to compound.

Pick a structured program and commit to it for at least 8–12 weeks before evaluating results. Anything less is not enough time to see what the program can actually do.

02 Skipping the Basics: Form Over Weight

Walking into the gym and immediately loading up the bar is a fast route to injury and slow progress. Beginners have a huge advantage that most people squander: their nervous system is fresh and highly responsive. In the first 3–6 months, your strength gains come almost entirely from neuromuscular adaptation, your brain learning to recruit muscles more efficiently, not from muscle growth itself.

This means the investment in learning correct form pays off massively. Perfect a squat, hinge, push, and pull pattern with light to moderate weight before you start loading. The gains come either way in the beginning, don't rush to weight that compromises technique and sets up injuries later.

  • Squat, deadlift, bench press, row, overhead press. Master these five movements and you have 90% of what you need.
  • Video yourself. What you feel and what's actually happening are often very different things.
  • Get a coach or experienced training partner to watch your form, even just for the first few weeks.

03 Doing Too Much Too Soon

Enthusiasm is not a training plan. Beginners who train 6 days a week immediately, doing 20 sets per muscle group, typically last 3–4 weeks before burnout, injury, or both. The gym humbles you fast when you ignore recovery.

As a beginner, you don't need a lot of volume to see results. Your body is so responsive to a new stimulus that even 3 days a week of full-body training is enough to produce rapid improvements in strength and physique. Start with less than you think you need. Add volume gradually as your recovery and conditioning improve.

04 Not Eating Enough Protein

Protein is the raw material your body uses to repair and build muscle tissue. Without adequate intake, training produces the stress, but the body can't adapt because it doesn't have the building blocks. This is an extremely common beginner blind spot.

Target at least 0.7–1g of protein per pound of bodyweight daily. For most Filipinos, this means significantly more protein than they're currently eating. Chicken, fish, eggs, pork, beef, tofu, and legumes are all solid sources. Protein shakes are useful but not mandatory if whole food sources are accessible.

  • Prioritize protein at every meal. Build the meal around your protein source first, then add carbs and fats.
  • Track for at least 2 weeks. Most people dramatically underestimate how much they eat and how little protein is actually in it.
  • Don't overthink food timing. Total daily protein matters far more than pre/post-workout timing for most people.

05 Comparing Your Progress to Others

The gym is not a competition. The person next to you has been training for 5 years. The physique you saw on Instagram is the product of years of work, optimal lighting, a good pump, and usually some editing. Comparing your month one to someone else's year five is a guaranteed way to demoralize yourself out of showing up.

Your only competition is who you were last week. Did you add weight to the bar? Did you complete more reps? Did you show up when you didn't feel like it? Those are the only metrics that matter at this stage.

06 Ignoring Sleep and Recovery

You grow outside the gym, not in it. Training creates the stimulus. Sleep is when your body responds to it. Beginners who train hard but sleep 5–6 hours consistently are actively working against themselves. Recovery is not optional, it's where the adaptation happens.

The fastest thing you can do to accelerate your results starting this week is get 7–9 hours of sleep consistently. No supplement, no program tweak, and no training hack comes close to what adequate sleep does for your body composition and performance.

07 Not Tracking Anything

If you're not tracking your lifts, you're not training, you're exercising. Training requires progression. Progression requires knowing where you started. A simple notebook or a notes app on your phone is all you need. Log the exercise, sets, reps, and weight for every session.

After a month, look back at week one. The progress will motivate you more than any video or post ever will, because it's yours. And it's real.
